Dr. Charles R. Drew

Surgeon, Doctor, Educator (1904–1950)

Dr. Charles Drew - Father of Modern Blood Banks

About the Father of the Modern Blood Bank

Birth
Charles Richard Drew was born on June 3, 1904, in Washington, D.C.
He was the son of Richard and Nora Drew and eldest of five children.
Education
Charles' was an excellent student, but he was also an outstanding athlete. He was an award winning swimmer and starred for his high school in football, baseball, basketball and track and field, winning the James E. Walker Memorial medal as his school’s best all around athlete.
He graduated from Dunbar High School in 1922.
He graduated with a bachelors degree from Amherst College in 1926.
Graduating from McGill University in 1933, Drew was second in his class and earned both Doctor of Medicine and Master of Surgery degrees.
In 1938, Drew received a Rockefeller Fellowship to study at Columbia University and train at the Presbyterian Hospital in New York City.
In 1940, he received his docorate degree. He became the first Black to receive a Doctor of Medical Science degree from Columbia
Career
In 1935, Charles began working as an instructor of pathology at Howard University in Washington, D.C.
At the same time, he was a resident at Freedmen’s Hospital.
In 1942, Drew returned to Howard University to head its Department of Surgery, as well as the Chief of Surgery at Freedmen’s Hospital.
Later he was named Chief of Staff and Medical Director for the Hospital.
In 1949 he was appointed Surgical Consultant for the United States Army’s European Theater of Operations.

Charles Drew - Medical Pioneer from biography.com.

Achievements
Dr. Drew developed a method for processing and preserving blood plasma, or blood without cells. Plasma lasts much longer than whole blood, making it possible to be stored or "banked" for longer periods of time
As World War II raged in Europe, Drew was asked to head up a special medical effort known as "Blood for Britain", an effort to transport desperately needed blood and plasma to Great Britain
He organized the collection and processing of blood plasma from several New York hospitals, and the shipments of these life-saving materials overseas to treat causalities in the war
Awards
In 1948 he was awarded the Spingarn Medal from the National Association for the Advancement of Colored People for his work on blood plasma.
He was also presented with the E. S. Jones Award for Research in Medical Science
He was the first Black to be appointed an examiner by the American Board of Surgery.
In 1945 he was presented honorary degrees of Doctor of Science from Virginia State College as well as Amherst College where he attended as an undergraduate student.
In 1946 he was elected Fellow of the International College of Surgeons.
Death
He died on April 1, 1950. Dr. Drew attended a medical conference at the Tuskegee Institute in Alabama, with three other doctors. He was behind the wheel when his vehicle crashed near Burlington, North Carolina. His passengers survived, but Dr. Charles R. Drew succumbed to his injuries.

As you know, there is no scientific basis for the separation of the bloods of different races except on the basis of the individual blood types or groups.
